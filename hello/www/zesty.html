It begins with cold, filtered Montana water, a blend of organic teas, organic cane sugar, and a symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast. Over 30 days, it’s passed down the row of fermenters, about once per week, or as often as batches are pulled from the end of the line. Along the way, it’s aging to perfection.

The kombucha spends it’s final week or two fermenting in oak barrels, soaking up the smooth, woody character and acid-balancing warmth of white American oak
